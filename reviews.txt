ASE 2026 Paper #4134 Reviews and Comments
===========================================================================
Paper #4134 An Empirical Study of GPU Kernel Performance Gaps in Modern
Domain-Specific Languages


Review #4134A
===========================================================================

Overall merit
-------------
2. Weak reject

Paper summary
-------------
This paper evaluates GPU kernel performance across two domain-specific languages (Triton and TileLang) and vendor libraries (cuBLAS and cuDNN) using 22 kernels spanning five operator categories. The study is structured around three research questions: (RQ1) quantifying the performance gap, (RQ2) analyzing its root causes, and (RQ3) assessing potential mitigations.

The results show that DSL performance is uneven. While it is competitive for element-wise and normalization operators, it lags significantly behind vendor libraries for convolution workloads. The authors attribute this gap to four main factors, which they analyze in detail. They further demonstrate that manual kernel rewrites can recover most of the lost performance for normalization, and improve Triton’s convolution performance to about 80% of cuDNN for one evaluated input shape.

Reasons to accept the paper
---------------------------
- The topic is timely. With Triton increasingly used as a default lowering target for `torch.compile`, understanding when and why DSL-generated kernels underperform relative to vendor libraries is both practically relevant and valuable.
- The overall methodology is reasonable in principle. The paper combines end-to-end latency measurements with hardware counter profiling, and attempts to validate hypotheses through targeted mitigations, although the execution and supporting evidence are uneven in places.
- The authors make reasonable efforts to control for confounding factors. For instance, they exclude attention workloads due to algorithmic non-equivalence, which helps keep comparisons fair and interpretable.

Reasons to not accept the paper
-------------------------------
- The paper does not clearly separate kernel authoring issues, example code quality, and compiler/code-generation limitations. For example, RC0 is framed as “TileLang Compiler-Level Deficiencies,” yet the evidence largely reflects the use of `T.serial` in example kernels, despite `T.reduce` being available. Similarly, the reported FP32 correctness issue is not traced to either the compiler or the kernel implementation. This conflation weakens the causal validity of the proposed root-cause taxonomy.
- The evaluation setup is not fully consistent across operator categories. Convolution is compared against cuDNN with best-algorithm selection (including Winograd), whereas normalization relies on PyTorch’s unfused eager implementation. This asymmetry limits cross-category comparability and makes aggregate metrics such as “Overall ∼65%” potentially misleading.
- The evidence supporting the identified root causes is uneven. Only RC1 and RC2 are backed by both profiling data and mitigation experiments. RC3 lacks profiling evidence for the filter sizes it aims to explain, and RC4 is inferred primarily by elimination rather than through controlled isolation. Although hardware counter analysis is introduced in Section 3.3, it is not consistently used to substantiate the claims.
-  While the paper shows that manual kernel rewrites can recover performance, it does not demonstrate corresponding improvements within the DSL frameworks themselves. As a result, it remains unclear whether the identified issues can be systematically addressed by the compiler or tooling.
- The experimental scope is limited. All results are obtained on a single GPU (RTX 4000 Ada), and a large portion of the evaluated kernels are relatively simple element-wise or reduction operators. Convolution, which is a central focus of the paper, is evaluated on only two input shapes with a single filter configuration (3×3, stride 1).

Detailed comments with constructive suggestions on how to improve the paper
---------------------------------------------------------------------------
### Overall Assessment

* **Significance:** The topic is timely and relevant, especially given Triton’s role in `torch.compile`. Understanding performance gaps between DSLs and vendor libraries has clear practical impact.

* **Novelty:** The paper provides a structured empirical comparison and attempts a root-cause-driven analysis. While the benchmarking itself is useful, the novelty mainly lies in the organization and interpretation rather than fundamentally new techniques.

* **Soundness:** The main concerns lie here. Several claims are not fully supported by consistent experimental coverage or clear attribution (e.g., conflation of compiler vs. kernel issues, limited convolution evaluation, and unvalidated root causes such as RC3). These issues reduce confidence in some of the conclusions.

### Evaluation and Characterization

* Section 3.1 claims coverage of Conv2d with filter sizes from 1×1 to 7×7, including depthwise and strided variants. However, Table 2 only reports results for 3×3, stride-1. The 1×1 case is deferred, and the 5×5/7×7 cases cited later (e.g., in RC3) are not evaluated. This weakens the empirical basis for some of the claims.

* Section 5 reports Δ = 0pp from heuristic tuning across all 22 kernels, yet Section 7.3 shows a 1.66× speedup for matmul when expanding the autotuning space. It is also unclear what “heuristically tuned” means in practice or what configurations were explored.

* Table 7 notes a 9% variation in baseline performance across profiling runs for the same convolution setup, attributed to GPU clock fluctuations. It is not stated whether clocks were locked. Without that, small efficiency differences (e.g., 94.6% vs. 97.8%) may not be meaningful.

### Root-Cause Analysis

* RC0 groups two different issues under “TileLang Compiler-Level Deficiencies”: (a) use of `T.serial` instead of `T.reduce`, which seems more like a kernel authoring or example issue, and (b) missing LDG.128 vectorized loads, which is more likely a code-generation limitation. Treating both as compiler issues overstates the compiler’s role.

* The FP32 correctness issue (99.6% incorrect outputs, very large max error) is reported without identifying whether it stems from the compiler, kernel logic, or configuration. Without that, it is hard to justify labeling it as a DSL-level problem.

* RC3 attributes performance loss for 5×5+ filters to register spilling and reduced occupancy, but such cases are not actually benchmarked, and no supporting data is shown. As presented, this reads more like a hypothesis than a validated finding.

* RC4 is plausible, but is inferred indirectly by attributing the remaining gap to missing Winograd optimizations. A more direct test (e.g., disabling Winograd in cuDNN) would make this claim more convincing.

* Section 3.3 introduces detailed Nsight Compute profiling, but the paper does not show the corresponding counter data. This makes it difficult to assess or verify the analysis.

* RC3 references A100 register characteristics, even though all experiments are run on Ada Lovelace (sm_89), which raises some concern about architectural relevance.

### Mitigation Analysis

* The convolution mitigation is evaluated on a single configuration only. Other shapes and filter sizes are not revisited, so it is unclear how broadly the result applies.

* The paper mentions that LayerNorm and argmax fixes required “18” and “13” iterations, but does not define what counts as an iteration (e.g., manual edits vs. tuning steps).

### Minor Issues

* Kernel count is inconsistent: “22 kernels” (abstract, Section 3) vs. “21 kernels” (Section 1).
* Typo in the abstract: “anamoly” → “anomaly.”

Questions for authors' response
-------------------------------
1. RC0 is described as “TileLang Compiler-Level Deficiencies,” yet the paper notes that `T.reduce` already exists and that the fix “does not represent a new optimization technique.” Could the authors clarify which components of RC0 stem from compiler/code-generation limitations versus kernel implementation or example code quality? Similarly, for the FP32 correctness failure (2067× max error), has the root cause been identified—i.e., whether it originates from the compiler, generated code, or kernel logic?

2. Section 3.1 states coverage of 1×1 to 7×7 filters (including depthwise and strided cases), but Table 2 only reports two 3×3, stride-1 configurations. Could the authors provide results for at least a 1×1 case (a useful control for isolating RC1) and a 5×5 case (relevant to RC3)? At present, some root-cause claims rely on configurations that are discussed but not evaluated.

3. Section 5 reports Δ = 0pp from heuristic tuning across all kernels, while Section 7.3 shows a 1.66× matmul speedup when expanding the autotuning space. Could the authors clarify what “heuristic tuning” entails (e.g., which parameters and ranges were explored), and why the expanded search from Section 7.3 was not applied in the main RQ1 evaluation?

4. Section 3.3 outlines a detailed Nsight Compute profiling methodology, but the paper does not present the corresponding counter data. Could the authors provide representative counter measurements supporting the root-cause analysis, such as vectorized load utilization for convolution vs. GEMM, register spill indicators for RC3, and warp stall breakdowns for RC0?

5. All experiments are conducted on a single RTX 4000 Ada GPU. Do the authors have evidence that the identified root causes and their relative impact generalize to data-center GPUs such as A100 or H100?

Review #4134B
===========================================================================

Overall merit
-------------
2. Weak reject

Paper summary
-------------
This paper presents the first study of performance gaps between GPU kernels with modern DSLs like Triton and TileLang, and optimized vendor libraries like cuBLAS and cuDNN. It reports performance differences across different categories and attempts to explain them through root-cause analysis and mitigations. However, the benchmark construction process is not clearly described, and the evaluation is not comprehensive enough. Several root-cause claims are not supported by direct experimental results.

Reasons to accept the paper
---------------------------
+ The topic is timely and important. Understanding whether GPU DSLs can match vendor libraries is important for DL application development.

+ The empirical results are valuable. The findings on performance gaps across Triton, TileLang, and libraries provide useful evidence for developers.

Reasons to not accept the paper
-------------------------------
- The benchmark construction process is not clearly described.

- The evaluation is incomplete.

- Some root-cause analysis lacks experimental support.

Detailed comments with constructive suggestions on how to improve the paper
---------------------------------------------------------------------------
1. The construction process of the benchmark is unclear. The authors state that the benchmark suite is derived from TritonBench, the TileLang example repository, and their own implementations for configurations not covered by either source. However, the TritonBench and TileLang example repository contains many kernels; it is unclear how the authors selected from them. It is also unclear how the authors decided which implementations to add. Moreover, the authors should justify why the resulting benchmark suite is representative.

2. The evaluation can be more comprehensive. First, while the authors state that there are Conv2d with 1 × 1 to 7 × 7 filters, Table 2 only presents the results for 3 × 3 filter. Second, while there are 15 element-wise tasks, the paper only reports overall results for this category, without results for each kernel.

3. Some root cause analysis is not supported by experiments. For example, Table 5 shows that the root causes (RC2b, RC3, and RC4) have no direct mitigation experiment conducted. While the analysis is reasonable, it is less convincing without experimental results. The authors should provide additional experimental results, or justify why such experiments are infeasible.

4. In RQ 1, the authors “re-evaluated all 22 kernels with heuristically tuned configurations for both DSLs”, but “every kernel returned identical efficiency to its default configuration (Δ = 0pp across all rows)” (lines 502-504). More details on the tuning process should be provided. For example, which parameters are tuned, and what is the search space? 

Minor issues
- Section 1 (line 138) says there are 21 kernels, but there are 22.
- The notations 16384² and 64 × 128² in Table 1 are not clear enough
- Typo in Abstraction: "anamoly" (line 27) should be "anomaly"?

Questions for authors' response
-------------------------------
1. How were kernels selected from TritonBench and the TileLang example repository? What was the criteria? How did the authors determine that the resulting kernel suite is representative?

2. Could the authors report the results on Conv2d with all filters from 1 × 1 to 7 × 7?

3. Could the authors report per-kernel results for the element-wise category?

4. Is it possible to do mitigation experiments for RC2b, RC3, and RC4?

5. How did the authors heuristically tune the configurations in RQ 1?



Review #4134C
===========================================================================

Overall merit
-------------
3. Weak accept

Paper summary
-------------
The paper presents an empirical study of performance gaps between GPU kernels written in modern DSLs, mainly Triton and TileLang, and library-backed PyTorch/cuBLAS/cuDNN baselines. The study covers 22 kernels across GEMM, attention, convolution, normalization, and element-wise/reduction categories on a single NVIDIA RTX 4000 Ada Generation GPU. The paper reports that Triton is competitive for several element-wise and normalization kernels but has large gaps on convolution and some GEMM shapes, while TileLang shows severe deficits on normalization and convolution. The authors use Nsight Compute counters to identify root causes and implement targeted mitigations that recover much of the gap for several kernels.

Reasons to accept the paper
---------------------------
- The topic is important for both SE and ML systems. Triton/TileLang-style DSLs are increasingly used in practice and in LLM-based kernel generation, so understanding when DSL kernels are actually production-competitive is valuable. The paper asks a clear empirical question and connects the results to practical debugging guidance.
- The methodology is stronger than a pure benchmark table: the authors combine latency measurements, hardware-counter profiling, root-cause analysis, and targeted mitigation experiments.
- The findings are concrete and useful, especially the distinction between GEMM/element-wise success cases and convolution/TileLang-normalization failure modes.

Reasons to not accept the paper
-------------------------------
- External validity is the main concern. The benchmark has only 22 kernels, usually two configurations per cell, one GPU architecture, and one software snapshot. 
- The study under-discusses correctness validation. A TileLang FP32 GEMM correctness failure is discovered and then excluded, but the paper gives limited detail on correctness tests, tolerances, and whether all measured kernels are semantically equivalent to the baselines.

Detailed comments with constructive suggestions on how to improve the paper
---------------------------------------------------------------------------
My main concern is the comprehensiveness of the study. The coverage of analysis is comparative narrow with respect to the claim.  The paper contains useful measurements, but the evaluated design space is small: 22 kernels, a single RTX 4000 Ada GPU, forward-pass kernels only, and mostly common deep-learning shapes. This is still valuable, but the claims should be calibrated accordingly.

Correctness validation should be described as a first-class part of the benchmark. Performance gaps only matter if all kernels compute the same function within a well-defined tolerance. The TileLang FP32 GEMM failure is actually an important SE finding, but excluding FP32 and moving on leaves questions: how many correctness failures occurred, what tolerances were used, were edge cases tested, and were the mitigation kernels revalidated?

The baseline construction is uneven across categories. GEMM and convolution have relatively clear cuBLAS/cuDNN-backed baselines. However, normalization and element-wise/reduction use PyTorch eager paths, which can be unfused and therefore much weaker than compiler-fused or vendor-optimized alternatives. The paper acknowledges the RMSNorm 1099% anomaly, but this issue affects the overall interpretation: a single metric named "library efficiency" means different things across categories. Authors may want to report separate metrics for vendor-library efficiency, eager-PyTorch efficiency, and compiler-fused baseline efficiency.

Questions for authors' response
-------------------------------
- How were correctness tests performed for all measured kernels and mitigation kernels? What tolerances, input distributions, and edge cases were used?
- Do the main conclusions hold on A100/H100 or another data-center GPU, especially for TileLang and convolution?
